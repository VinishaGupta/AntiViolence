GENDER INEQUALITY

Gender inequality is the social phenomenon in which people are not treated equally on the basis of gender. This inequality can be caused by gender discrimination or sexism. The treatment may arise from distinctions regarding biology, psychology, or cultural norms prevalent in the society. Some of these distinctions are empirically grounded, while others appear to be social constructs. While current policies around the world cause inequality among individuals, it is women who are most affected. Gender inequality weakens women in many areas such as health, education, and business life. Studies show the different experiences of genders across many domains including education, life expectancy, personality, interests, family life, careers, and political affiliation. Gender inequality is experienced differently across different cultures and also affects non-binary people.

GENDER INEQUALITY INDEX (GII)

GII is a composite metric of gender inequality using three dimensions: reproductive health, empowerment and the labour market. A low GII value indicates low inequality between women and men, and vice-versa.

About GII

GII reflects gender-based disadvantage in three dimensions— reproductive health, empowerment and the labour market—for as many countries as data of reasonable quality allow. It shows the loss in potential human development due to inequality between female and male achievements in these dimensions. It ranges from 0, where women and men fare equally, to 1, where one gender fares as poorly as possible in all measured dimensions. GII values are computed using the association-sensitive inequality measure suggested by Seth (2009), which implies that the index is based on the general mean of general means of different orders—the first aggregation is by a geometric mean across dimensions; these means, calculated separately for women and men, are then aggregated using a harmonic mean across genders.

Women’s Rights in India:

Gender inequality and discrimination against women are pervasive problems in many societies, including India. Despite constitutional protections and legal frameworks, women in India continue to face various forms of gender-based discrimination and violence. However, there have been some positive developments in recent years, including greater awareness and advocacy for women’s rights, as well as the involvement of lawyers for women’s rights in promoting gender equality and justice.

Historical Context of Gender Equality in India

Gender equality in India is deeply rooted in its rich history, stretching back centuries. The ancient scriptures and epics of India, like the Mahabharata and Ramayana, depict women as powerful figures, both revered and feared. However, as time progressed, the status of women saw a decline, especially during the medieval and colonial periods. Practices like purdah (seclusion of women), child marriage, and Sati (the burning of the widow on her husband’s pyre) became prevalent. It wasn’t until the 19th century that reformers like Raja Ram Mohan Roy and Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar started advocating for women’s rights, leading to the abolition of Sati and the introduction of widow remarriage. In the 20th century, leaders like Mahatma Gandhi promoted women’s participation in the freedom struggle, further elevating their status in society. The post-independence era saw the Constitution of India grant equal rights to women, setting the stage for their progress in contemporary times.

Legal Framework for Women’s Rights in India:

The Constitution of India guarantees gender equality and prohibits discrimination on the basis of gender. Several laws have been enacted to protect women’s rights, including the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition, and Redressal) Act, and the Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, among others.

However, the implementation of these laws has been challenging, with various social, cultural, and economic factors contributing to the persistence of gender-based discrimination and violence. The lack of legal literacy and access to justice also pose significant barriers for women seeking to assert their rights.

Challenges in Implementing Women’s Rights Laws

The Indian legal system has a comprehensive framework for protecting women’s rights, but implementing these laws effectively remains a significant challenge. Factors contributing to this include deep-seated societal norms, lack of awareness about legal rights among women, and inadequate law enforcement. For instance, the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act provides a mechanism for women to seek legal recourse against abuse, but due to societal stigma and fear of social ostracism, many women hesitate to come forward. Additionally, the judicial process can be lengthy and cumbersome, discouraging victims from pursuing legal action. Another significant issue is the underreporting of crimes against women, often influenced by the victim’s fear of retaliation or family honor. Grassroots level education and awareness, along with more efficient legal processes, are essential to overcome these challenges.

Women’s Rights and Gender Inequality in India:

Despite the legal protections in place, women in India continue to face various forms of gender-based violence and discrimination. Some of the most pressing issues include domestic violence, sexual harassment, gender-based violence, discrimination in education and employment, and unequal access to resources and opportunities.

Domestic violence is a pervasive problem in India, with a large percentage of women experiencing physical, sexual, and emotional abuse at the hands of their intimate partners. The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act provides legal recourse for women facing domestic violence, but the implementation of the act has been uneven.

Sexual harassment is another major issue, with many women experiencing harassment and assault in public spaces, workplaces, and educational institutions. The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act provides legal protections for women facing sexual harassment at work, but many women are still reluctant to report harassment due to fear of retaliation and social stigma.

Gender-based violence, including rape, sexual assault, and female infanticide, are also prevalent in India. The legal framework for addressing gender-based violence is weak and often fails to hold perpetrators accountable.

Discrimination in education and employment is another major barrier to gender equality in India. Many girls are denied access to education, and women often face discrimination and harassment in the workplace. The lack of economic opportunities for women perpetuates gender inequality and reinforces social and cultural norms that devalue women’s contributions and capabilities.

How Can Society Support Gender Equality?

The journey towards achieving gender equality is not just a legal battle but also a societal one. Every individual can contribute to this cause in multiple ways. Firstly, education plays a crucial role. Educating both boys and girls about gender equality from a young age can foster a culture of mutual respect and understanding. Secondly, challenging stereotypes and biases in everyday life, whether in the household, workplace, or public spaces, can create an environment conducive to gender equality. Support for women’s rights also includes advocating for policies that promote gender parity and supporting women in leadership roles. Moreover, men as allies in the fight for gender equality can significantly impact societal attitudes and practices. Lastly, supporting NGOs and initiatives that work towards women’s empowerment can help bring about systemic change.

HelpLines

In India, gender inequality is a persistent issue that affects many aspects of society, including education, employment, healthcare, and legal rights. To help address these issues and support those affected, various helplines and organizations offer assistance and resources:

  1. National Commission for Women (NCW):
    • Helpline Number: 7827170170
    • Offers support and guidance to women facing discrimination and injustice.
  2. Women Helpline (WHL):
    • Helpline Number: 181
    • Provides support for women in distress, including legal and psychological help.
  3. Childline India:
    • Helpline Number: 1098
    • Focuses on providing assistance to children facing abuse, exploitation, and other issues.
  4. National Domestic Abuse Helpline (State-specific):
    • Many states in India have their own domestic abuse helpline numbers. Check for the specific number in your state.
  5. National Legal Services Authority (NALSA):
    • Helpline Number: 15100
    • Offers free legal services to women and others in need.
  6. Shakti Shalini:
    • Helpline Number: 10920
    • Provides support and counselling for survivors of violence.
  7. Snehi Helpline:
    • Helpline Number: 9820466726
    • Offers counseling and support for women facing emotional distress or abuse.

These helplines can provide immediate support and assistance for those dealing with gender inequality and related issues in India.
